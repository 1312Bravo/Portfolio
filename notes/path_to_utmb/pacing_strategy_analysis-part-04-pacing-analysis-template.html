Pacing analysis

At this point, we have two normalized quantities:

  • Normalized race time, which tells us whether a runner finished better or worse than expected, given their ability and race edition.
  • Normalized sector pacing, which tells us whether a runner spent more or less of their race than expected in each sector, given the sector, year, and ability baseline.

Together, these let us ask the real pacing question: which ways of distributing effort through UTMB are associated with better race outcomes?

The goal is not to find the fastest raw sectors. Faster runners are usually faster everywhere. The goal is to understand whether the runners who performed better than expected followed a different pacing pattern from those who performed worse than expected. Did they lose less time late? Did they avoid spending too much of the race in the early climbs? Did they distribute their effort more evenly? Or is the classic idea that “the winner is the one who slows down the least” too simple?

From sectors to race phases

Sector-level pacing is detailed, but it can also be noisy. UTMB has many checkpoint-to-checkpoint sectors, and each one has its own distance, terrain, and race context. Looking at every sector separately is useful for exploration, but it can make the story hard to read.

So before the main pacing analysis, I group sectors into broader race phases. A phase is a larger part of the race made from several neighboring sectors. This lets us keep the main race structure while reducing noise from individual checkpoints.

The idea is to move from this:

  • sector 1
  • sector 2
  • sector 3
  • …

to something more readable:

  • early race
  • middle race
  • late race
  • final section

The exact phase definitions should still respect the course. They should follow meaningful parts of the route, not arbitrary equal-distance bins. Ideally, each phase should reflect a different race context: early positioning, the first major mountain blocks, the night or middle-race fatigue section, and the late-race survival or closing section.

Phase-level pacing

Once sectors are grouped into phases, we can summarize sector-level pacing into phase-level pacing.

The simplest version is:

\[ \text{Phase pacing} = \text{average normalized sector pacing within the phase} \]

This gives each runner one pacing value per phase. A positive value means the runner spent less time share than expected in that phase. A negative value means the runner spent more time share than expected.

This phase-level view is easier to interpret than individual sectors. Instead of saying a runner was stronger than expected between two specific checkpoints, we can say they spent less than expected in the early race, or more than expected in the late race. That is closer to how pacing is usually discussed in real racing.

There is one important note: phase-level pacing still depends on the sector normalization. It is not raw speed and it is not absolute effort. It is a relative measure of where the runner spent more or less of their race than expected.

First look: performance groups

The first analysis should be simple and visual.

We split runners into five performance groups based on normalized race time:

  • much worse than expected
  • worse than expected
  • around expected
  • better than expected
  • much better than expected

Then we compare how these groups distributed their pacing across race phases.

This gives the first answer to the main question: do better-than-expected runners have a visibly different pacing pattern from worse-than-expected runners?

What to look for:

  • Do the best groups avoid large negative pacing values late in the race?
  • Do the worst groups spend too much time share in the second half?
  • Are better outcomes linked with a conservative start, a strong finish, or simply less late-race collapse?
  • Is the difference visible already in the early phases, or does it appear mostly later?

This section should stay descriptive. It should help the reader see the pacing story before we move into feature engineering and modeling.

Deeper analysis: pacing features

After the group-level view, we turn the phase and sector metrics into pacing features. These features should describe different ideas of pacing, not just repeat the same number many times.

Possible feature groups:

  • Early pacing: how aggressive the runner was in the first part of the race.
  • Late pacing: whether the runner lost more or less time than expected later.
  • Pacing fade: the difference between early and late pacing.
  • Pacing variability: whether the runner’s sector pacing was smooth or uneven.
  • Climb / descent pattern: whether the runner performed differently on uphill, downhill, or flatter/runnable sectors.
  • Phase balance: whether the runner’s time loss was concentrated in one phase or spread across the race.

The goal is not to create as many features as possible. The goal is to create features that correspond to real pacing ideas:

  • starting too hard
  • slowing down less
  • losing time late
  • being consistent
  • handling climbs or descents better
  • avoiding one bad section that ruins the whole race

Univariate analysis

Before building a larger model, we first look at each pacing feature on its own.

The question here is simple: when this feature increases, do runners tend to finish better or worse than expected?

This can be done with simple correlations, plots, or one-feature regressions. The point is not to claim causality. It is to understand which pacing features have a visible relationship with normalized race performance.

This section should answer:

  • Which pacing features have the clearest relationship with performance?
  • Are the signs intuitive?
  • Does late-race pacing matter more than early pacing?
  • Is pacing variability harmful?
  • Does any feature contradict the simple “slow down the least” hypothesis?

The univariate step is useful because it keeps the interpretation transparent. If a feature has no visible relationship on its own, we should be careful about over-interpreting it later in a multivariate model.

Multivariate analysis

The multivariate analysis asks a stricter question: which pacing features still matter when we consider them together?

This is important because pacing features are likely correlated. For example, a runner with strong late pacing may also have a smaller early-to-late fade. A runner with very uneven pacing may also have one bad late phase. Looking at features one at a time can overstate their independent importance.

The model should predict normalized race time from the pacing features:

\[ \text{Normalized race time} \sim \text{pacing features} \]

The goal is not to build a perfect prediction model. The goal is to identify which pacing patterns are most consistently associated with better-than-expected race performance.

This section should explain:

  • which features were included
  • why they were included
  • how correlated or redundant features were handled
  • which features remained important
  • what the final model suggests about pacing

The final interpretation should come back to the original hypothesis:

Is the best pacing strategy the one that preserves speed relative to ability for as long as possible?

Possible conclusion directions:

  • If late pacing / low fade dominates, the classic saying is supported.
  • If early restraint is more important, the story is more about avoiding early damage.
  • If variability matters, the story is about avoiding large pacing disruptions.
  • If climb/descent features matter, the story may be course-specific rather than only fatigue-based.
  • If effects are weak, then pacing may matter, but the data cannot separate it cleanly from ability, terrain, and race execution.
